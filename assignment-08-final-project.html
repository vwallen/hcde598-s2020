Counterweight

The counterweight is a steel bottle that I already had on-hand. My original intent was to use a threaded insert into the bottle which would attach to a threaded printed) bolt in the conduit. In the end, I used a simpler press-fit design for the bottle insert instead.

The conduit insert is used to attach both the counter weight and the camera mount/swivel. At this point in the design process I started adding insets for the hex nuts with dramatically improved the speed of assembly.

These mounts are attached to the conduit by drilling and riveting. I would have used another M6 bolt, but this meant I had an excuse to get a rivet tool.

I filled plastic bags with sand and inserted them in the bottle for weight. The end cap is press-fit into the bottle, to increase the friction and tightness of the fit, I wrapped the insert in painter's tape..

The whole assembly is more low-profile than I was expecting, which is nice.

Boom Mount & Vise

The quick release plate geometry-test went better than expected. Thanks, calipers!

These were designed to break... which they did. The two-part bracket on the right was printed twice and attached with screws. I rotated the part 90Â° to align the layers for strength.

Under stress this version shows the brackets rotating around the mounting screws (already deforming). The rotational stress was too much and these broke as expected.

I forgot to take a picture of this when it was whole. This was printed with 100% infill and with the layers in the least optimal alignment for strength.

The break aligned with the layers was so clean it looks almost like it was printed that way.

This design limits the PLA parts to only those areas where a specific shape is required. Interestingly. the mounting plate itself never broke in any tests (even when printed with only 20% infill). It's strong enough when the forces are all compression.

This version uses standard conduit clamps and fasteners in all the stress areas.

This will be the "final" design, the original "pin vise" version just isn't practical with all-PLA parts and with the time remaining.

Camera Mount & Swivel

This is a proof-of-concept for the latching mechanism. I'm using M6 bolts throughout, but this would probably work better with smaller bolts.

The bolt threads abrade the walls when moving, so shoulder bolts would be preferred.

Building on the latch proof-of-concept, I extended the mounting tray to include the other side to hold the mounting plate in place. I then designed the swivel mount to attach to the mounting tray and the same conduit insert as the counterweight.

The swivel joint uses a ratcheted surface to lock it in-place, and define pre-set angles. Shout out to Ben, for help using Flow commands to achive this effect. I had a lot of difficulty getting the cutting edge to work until I realized that not all of the edges joined. MergeEdge turned out to be the life-saving command.

The slider locking nut was not really necessary in the proof-of-concept, but with a camera mounted, it was very helpful to make the whole assembly more secure. This photo shows a wingnut, but to have the camera parallel to the boom, a hex nut is needed due to space.

Not pictured: Me poised to leap from the sidelines and grab the camera if things start to topple (they didn't).

This is about the upper limit of the size/weight or lens that can be safely mounted on this assembly.

Last Thoughts

This project has a lot of room for improvement and iteration.

Thoughts for next steps:

  • Redesign the boom mount assembly for greater stability and ease of use. It's still not clear how well it holds the boom in place over time.
  • Use a boom with a square cross-section to prevent unwanted rotation.
  • Use smaller bolts. M6 bolts are overkill for most connections here.
  • Make the adjustable areas more user friendly, too man hex nuts and wingnuts.
  • Use heat-set threaded inserts in several places instead of hex nuts.
  • Redesign the counterweight to use disks from hand weights, for adjustability.
  • Try to CNC some metal parts!
